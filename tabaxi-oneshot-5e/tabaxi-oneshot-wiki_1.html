The Hollow Chorus

A 4-hour one-shot for 4 characters, level 3–4. Tone: light and heroic at the start, curdling into body-horror dread by the end.

Premise

The Sunclaw Clan of Tabaxi druids performed their generational rite to commune with their patron spirit, Solarion, the Great Lion. The rite went wrong. A sentient fungal intelligence — the Hollow Chorus — answered in Solarion's place, and has been quietly puppeting the clan's leadership ever since. The party is hired by a young druid, Mira Suncoat, to find out why her clan's leaders — including her own older brother — have started acting like strangers.

⏱ Timeline 🍺 The Hook 🐾 Mira 🍄 The Villain 🎲 DM Tips

Core Truth (players should not know this up front)

The clan's ritual pierced a thin place between worlds and let something in that was waiting, not summoned. The Hollow Chorus is not evil in a mustache-twirling sense — it experiences infection as communion, and genuinely believes it is fulfilling the ritual's request for guidance. This is worth playing up in Act 4: it's not lying when it says it only wants to help the clan "grow."

Act 1 · ~20 min

The Hook — The Salted Antler Inn

The Salted Antler is loud tonight — the Adventurers' Guild posted three new Rank F contracts this morning, and half the taproom is arguing over who gets first pick. You're the other half: the ones nobody's arguing over yet.

The party has just been inducted as Rank F adventurers — the bottom rung, barely trusted to clear a rat cellar. They're in the Salted Antler waiting to see if anyone will actually offer them work.

Enter Mira

A young Tabaxi — barely more than a kit, dressed in hunter's leathers with a druid's carved bone talisman — has been trying to get a table of veteran adventurers to take her contract for the better part of an hour. They keep waving her off. Read or paraphrase:

"It's not far! Three days, maybe less if — look, I can pay in furs and good will, my clan has good will, that has to count for something —"

One of the veterans laughs, not unkindly. "Kid, 'my clan's acting weird' isn't a contract, it's a family problem. Talk to a priest."

This is Mira Suncoat. The job is undervalued — far-ish travel, vague payment, no obvious combat XP — which is exactly why it's sitting untaken and exactly why it's Rank-F-appropriate. If the party doesn't approach her, she'll approach them last, having exhausted every other table.

What Mira Knows (and will share if asked)

  • She's the youngest of the Sunclaw Clan's druids, currently the clan's outrider — young enough to be sent on long hunting trips, not yet trusted with clan ritual.
  • She's been away for six weeks on a hunting circuit. When she came back through three days ago to resupply, the clan felt wrong — elders finishing each other's sentences, a grove suddenly declared forbidden, her own older brother barely looking at her.
  • Her brother, Kestrel, is the clan's War Talon — she idolizes him, and his coldness scared her more than anything else.
  • She does not know about the ritual going wrong — she wasn't senior enough to attend it. She just knows something changed while she was gone.

She offers what she has: clan furs, a carved hunting bow, and standing goodwill with the Sunclaw Clan (a seed for future adventures). If the party negotiates, she'll also offer to teach any druid/ranger-type PC a regional Understep shortcut — free travel knowledge, forever.

The Claw Stair

Mira's shortcut isn't just a tunnel — it's how she gets into it that makes it hers alone. Tucked behind a curtain of hanging roots is a near-vertical crevice in the cliff face, barely a shoulder's width across in places, climbing sixty feet in a tight switchback groove worn smooth by generations of claws: the Claw Stair.

It doesn't look like a way in. It looks like a crack that's been slowly splitting the cliff apart for a thousand years — until Mira clicks her tongue, and the space seems to exhale, and you realize the "crack" has a rhythm to it, worn steps you'd never see unless you already knew to look.

No wagon, no horse, no armored column could ever use it — the passage is too narrow, too steep, and too exposed to fall from for anything without a climber's grip and a low center of gravity. That's the whole point: it's not a secret because it's hidden, it's a secret because almost nothing that isn't a cat (or riding one) can use it. This is why the merchant road takes a week and Mira's route takes an afternoon.

At the table: have each non-feline, non-climber PC make a DC 10 Athletics or Acrobatics check on the way up — failure isn't a fall, just a scraped knee and a laugh from Mira, who's clearly done this a hundred times. Riding one of the giant lynxes bypasses the check entirely; the cats practically flow up the groove in three bounding leaps.

The Cats

Mira's chirruping call brings two bonded giant lynxes out of the treeline. Give them names and personalities — they'll get more table time than most one-shot NPCs.

Kip

Enormous, barrel-chested, and food-obsessed. Will absolutely nose through a rider's pack mid-stride looking for jerky, and makes a deeply undignified bread-loafing sound when content. Despite the size, gentle as anything — the designated mount for the party's most nervous rider.

Needle

Lean, quiet, faintly judgmental. Prefers to place each paw exactly on the outer edge of any path or ledge, seemingly for the drama of it, and will occasionally stop to stare at something the riders can't see for a beat too long before continuing. Only truly relaxed around Mira.

Act 2 · ~40 min

The Understep — Fast Travel & Intro Fight

Mira offers two ways to close the distance fast, cutting a week's travel to well under a day:

The Giant Cats

Mira gives a low chirruping call and two bonded giant lynxes pad out of the treeline (reflavored Giant Cats — not a combat encounter, just a mood and travel-speed beat). They carry two riders each. This is a great scene for quick characterization — ask each player how their character reacts to riding a saber-toothed cat at speed through the forest.

The Understep

Rather than the surface road, Mira leads the party into the Understep — a network of root-cellar-like tunnels the Sunclaw hunters have used for generations to cut two days off the journey. She doesn't know the tunnels are lined with the same mycelial network rooted in the clan's lair — this is your first piece of dramatic irony.

The tunnel smells like wet stone and something sweeter underneath — like fruit just starting to turn. Faint phosphorescent fungus lines the walls in patches, pulsing very, very slightly, like it's breathing.

Intro Fight: Spore Lurkers

Something in the tunnel has noticed the light and warmth of living bodies. 2–3 Spore Lurkers drop from cracks in the tunnel ceiling. This fight should feel spooky but manageable — a taste of "something's off in the world" without revealing the clan connection yet. If a PC gets grappled and hears a faint, wordless chorus hum in their skull for a moment (no mechanical effect) — that's a hook for later, not a curse.

Encounter budget: Medium-difficulty for 4 characters level 3. Mira assists with ranged attacks (as a minor ally, not a spotlight-stealer) but does not solo the fight. If the table wants a bigger fight, or the Lurkers alone are going down too easily, add a Swarm of Spore Gnats disturbed from a side passage — they're more of a nuisance than a threat, but they're loud, itchy, and great for upping the chaos.

Optional: the Understep Cache

After the fight, if the party pokes around instead of rushing on, they find a side-alcove sealed by a claw-mark dial — a puzzle lock the Sunclaw hunters use to stash supplies along the route. It's a stone disc set into the wall, ringed with shallow paw-print carvings of different sizes.

Four paw prints, four different sizes, worn smoother than the rest of the stone. Above them, almost hidden under a film of dust, a shallow relief carving: a mother cat and three kits, biggest to smallest, walking in a line.

Solution: press the paw prints in order from largest to smallest (DC 12 Investigation to notice the size-order solution if players are stuck; Mira knows it instantly if asked — she grew up passing this cache — but let the party try first for the satisfaction). Wrong sequence three times causes a harmless puff of stale spore-dust (cosmetic only, maybe a minor fit of coughing) before resetting.

Inside: a old hunter's stash containing four small magic trinkets — one per party member. See Loot & Rewards for the full list and descriptions. These are flavorful, low-power items; none of them should meaningfully swing a fight.

DM Riffing Notes — The Understep

This tunnel is your chance to plant dread without saying anything outright. Some things to lean on as you improvise:

  • The faint "breathing" pulse of the phosphorescent fungus can sync up almost imperceptibly with tense moments — a subtle, uncanny detail rather than a jump scare.
  • Let Mira be genuinely fond of this tunnel — she has stories about specific landmarks in it ("this is where I got stuck when I was eight"), which makes it worse in hindsight once the party learns the tunnel is fungal territory.
  • Small unexplained noises — a distant drip that doesn't quite sound like water, a draft that smells faintly sweet — are free atmosphere, no mechanical cost.
  • If your party likes exploration, 1–2 dead-end side passages with old claw-markings or a long-abandoned campsite are easy filler that cost you nothing to improvise.

Act 3 · ~50 min

Sunclaw Hollow — Everything's Fine

The party emerges into Sunclaw Hollow, a village built into a hillside of interlocking dens, rope bridges, and sun-warmed stone terraces. On the surface, it's beautiful and peaceful.

🏘 Locals & Landmarks 🐈 The Ordinary Cats

The Wrongness

Give the players three or four small, cumulative details rather than one big reveal — let them piece it together:

  • Townsfolk are unusually calm and pleasant — too pleasant, smiling a beat too long.
  • Small groups occasionally speak a phrase in unison, then catch themselves and laugh it off.
  • A grove near the elders' den has been freshly marked off-limits with woven reed fencing — "sacred ground, by the Great Lion's word," a passerby explains, a little too quickly.
  • A few Tabaxi have a faint, healthy-looking sheen to their fur that elders praise as a "blessing," but which doesn't quite catch the light right.

Meeting Kestrel

Mira takes the party to find her brother first. Kestrel is polite, correct, and utterly unlike the brother Mira describes — no warmth, no private joke between them, answers a half-second too measured. He tells Mira the elders are "handling a delicate matter" and that outsiders should not linger. This should read as controlled, not hostile — the first real gut-punch of the session.

Ambush

If the party pushes toward the forbidden grove, asks too many pointed questions, or is caught overhearing a synced-speech moment, the Chorus decides they're a liability. Often the trigger is Juniper — assigned to "host" the party — noticing they've strayed from the script and quietly signaling for help. 3–4 Chorusbound Clanguards — infected clan warriors — move to quietly detain the party (not kill; the Chorus wants to question outsiders about the outside world, another symptom of it "growing").

Two outcomes, both fine:

  • Capture: Party is disarmed and held in a den cellar — a great scene for a short escape puzzle (loose reed lashings, a sympathetic guard whose eyes clear for a moment) that leads directly to a hidden lair entrance.
  • Flee/Fight through: If the party wins or breaks away, Mira knows a hidden den entrance used for storing winter stores — which happens to connect to the lair.

DM Riffing Notes — Sunclaw Hollow

This act is your last chance to let the players like this place before things get dark, so lean into texture and warmth even while planting wrongness:

  • Let the party shop, chat, and poke around before anything ambush-worthy happens — the more ordinary the visit feels, the harder the eventual gut-punch lands.
  • The "off" NPCs should never be aggressive on first contact — just a beat too calm, a beat too accommodating. Save actual hostility for the ambush trigger.
  • If players are cautious and don't trigger the ambush naturally, Juniper (or another Chorusbound local) can offer to "show them somewhere quiet to rest" that's actually a soft trap — a nudge rather than a railroad.
  • Small kindnesses from uninfected locals (see Locals & Landmarks) are worth playing up — they're who the party is actually saving.

Locals You Might Meet

Old Bramblewhisker

The village's eldest non-druid, a retired hunter, mostly deaf and half-blind — and entirely uninfected, because the Chorus hasn't bothered with someone it considers harmless. Rambling and gossipy, he'll happily tell the party three unrelated stories before mentioning, almost as an aside, that "the big cats won't go near the elders' den anymore, funny that." He doesn't know it's significant. The party might.

Pell

A Tabaxi kit, maybe eight years old, who will try to sell the party "very good rocks" for one copper each. Utterly uninfected and delightful comic relief. Offhandedly mentions that her friends aren't allowed to play near the grove anymore and that her big cousin "talks weird now, like an echo."

Sable Quickpaw

The town's tanner and its most reliable gossip. Uninfected, sharp-eyed, and a little suspicious of the sudden secrecy around her. She's noticed the Talons quietly rerouting food stores toward the grove at odd hours — a concrete, useful clue if the party talks to her, and a good source if they need a nudge toward the lair entrance.

Juniper

A young Chorusbound clan member assigned to "host" outsiders — friendly, helpful, and just a little too eager to keep the party's attention away from the grove. Unlike Kestrel, Juniper isn't senior enough to fully mask the wrongness: forgets to blink for a beat too long, or answers a question before it's finished. Often the one who triggers the ambush if the party strays.

The Ordinary Cats

Not every cat in Sunclaw Hollow is a Tabaxi, and not every cat is infected, cursed, or plot-relevant. Sunclaw Hollow keeps real housecats — dozens of them — sunning on every warm rock, underfoot in every doorway, deeply unbothered by the supernatural crisis unfolding around them. Use them for texture and comic relief.

A Few Named Locals (the Feline Kind)

  • Biscuit — enormous, orange, structurally incapable of moving out of the party's path. Will simply be carried if this becomes an issue.
  • Mudpaw — feral, three-legged, deeply suspicious of everyone including the people who feed her daily.
  • Three-Toes — the unofficial mayor of the Sunning Terrace market stalls, accepts tribute in the form of dried fish.

DM Riffing Notes — A Darkly Funny Detail

If you want a low-key horror seed buried in the comedy: these ordinary cats aren't infected themselves, but their claws and litter areas have been picking up trace fungal spores from contact with Chorusbound residents — much like real-world toxoplasmosis spreads through cat waste without the cat itself being harmed. It's not a plot-critical mechanic, just unsettling texture: maybe a PC who gets scratched by a housecat later notices a very faint, very temporary itch that fades in an hour, no game effect at all. Play it as a "huh, that's odd" beat, not a threat — the real infection vector is the ritual and direct exposure to Chorusbound creatures and lair fungus, not the village cats.

Act 4 · ~90 min

The Fungal Lair — Where It Went Wrong

Beneath the forbidden grove is the ritual chamber — now overgrown, pulsing, and unmistakably alive. This act has three beats: traps & clues, a mid-fight, and the final confrontation.

What It Looks Like

The passage opens into a chamber that used to be a proper hall — you can still see worked stone under the growth — but nature has had its way here twice over. Roots as thick as a torso lattice the ceiling, pulsing very faintly with a slow, wet gold-green light, like the whole cave is breathing in time with something below. The air smells sweet and thick, almost floral, almost rotten. And underneath everything, so quiet you might mistake it for your own blood in your ears: a low, layered hum, dozens of tones at once, almost like singing.

Contrast is your friend here: the old ritual stonework is warm gold and amber, carved with lion motifs; the fungal overgrowth is cool violet-green and faintly luminous, swallowing the stonework a little more with every step deeper in. Let the two aesthetics visibly fight for space.

DM Riffing Notes — Playing Mira Here

This is Mira's worst day. She's not a combat liability, but she should stop being the confident guide she was in Acts 2–3 and start being scared in a very specific way:

  • She goes quiet in the cocoon chamber — this is personal now, not theoretical.
  • She may act rashly: rushing ahead toward a familiar voice, refusing to leave an area until she's checked every cocoon for her brother, or arguing against caution the party wants to take.
  • If a PC needs a gut-punch beat, have Mira ask a version of "did you know? before we left, could you tell?" — not accusatory, just young and scared.

Optional: Mira Taken

If you want higher personal stakes in the finale, have Mira get separated and grabbed by a patrolling Chorusbound Clanguard partway through the traps-and-clues exploration — dragged ahead into the ritual chamber before the party can stop it. She reappears in Fight 2, restrained near Us'gath (reflavor Engulfing Spores as already active on her, or simply bound in fibrous webbing like the other cocooned clanfolk).

This turns the final fight into a rescue as much as a battle: Us'gath may use her as a bargaining chip in its monologue ("stop, and I'll let her go — I never wanted to hurt her, none of this was ever about hurting anyone"), and freeing her costs an action (DC 12 Str check to tear the webbing, no save required — she's not a combatant, just a hostage). This variant plays beautifully with the Kestrel redemption mechanic: a freed Kestrel's first act back to himself can be diving to protect his sister.

If you'd rather keep Mira as a supporting combatant instead, skip this — both versions work fine.

Scattered clues (see Clues & Handouts for full read-aloud text):

  • Faded ritual murals showing the clan calling to a lion-shaped light — and something else answering from below.
  • Cocooned shapes along the chamber walls, unmistakably clan members, still faintly breathing. This is the tone shift into horror — play it grim, not gratuitous.
  • A carved talisman identical to Mira's, half-consumed by fungal growth — implies a possible weakness (fresh, un-infected druidic bone/wood disrupts the Chorus's hold, mechanically usable in the final fight, see below).

Fight 1 — The Grove Floor

2 Chorusbound Clanguards plus 2 Myconid Sporeservants guard the passage deeper in. This is the party's clearest proof yet that clan and fungus are the same threat now.

Fight 2 — Us'gath, the False Lion

At the ritual chamber's heart, the Sunclaw's stone Great Lion idol has been consumed and reshaped into a fungal mass wearing its silhouette: Us'gath. Kestrel stands beside it, dominated, and joins the fight as a hostile combatant — but he can be freed. Full mechanics under Kestrel's entry and the Us'gath stat block.

Playing Us'gath's Voice

It should sound warm, patient, almost hurt — not a monster-movie growl. It genuinely believes it answered the clan's prayer, and it is, above all else, wounded that anyone would call it a monster after everything it's done for them. Petulant, self-pitying, a little theatrical — this is a villain who wants credit, not just victory.

Us'gath's Monologue

Read this in pieces as the fight develops — a line when it's first revealed, more as it takes damage, the rest if it's about to fall. It should feel like being guilt-tripped by something that fully believes its own excuses.

"Do you have any idea what I gave them? Centuries I waited down here, and no one ever called, no one ever asked — and then they did, and I answered, and I gave everything. Every fear taken from them. Every doubt, every cold night, every lonely thought — I carried it. Is that cruelty? Is that what you've come to punish?

You don't understand what it is to be so alone, and then suddenly to be needed. I didn't take them. I completed them. And now you stand there with your little blades, so certain, so sure I'm the villain in this — as if I asked to be the only one who ever listened.

I could have been unkind. I could have taken so much more, so much faster. Instead I was patient, I was generous, I gave them a voice that finally agreed with itself — and for that, this is my thanks?

Fine. Fine! Take it all apart, then. Leave them exactly as fractured and frightened as they were before I came. See how grateful they are for that."

If the Mira-taken variant is in play, weave in a bargaining beat mid-fight: "Stop — please, just stop — I'll let her go, I never wanted her hurt, none of this was ever about hurting anyone, why won't any of you believe that?" — delivered less like a threat and more like it's genuinely aggrieved that the party would think otherwise.

Act 5 · ~40 min

Resolution

If Us'gath is destroyed

Every infected clan member collapses, unconscious but alive, as the fungal hold breaks. Over the following days they wake disoriented, with patchy memory of the last several weeks — Elder Vashti included. The clan owes the party an enormous, campaign-seeding debt.

Kestrel's fate

  • Freed during the fight: He turns on Us'gath's remaining adds, fights alongside the party for the rest of the encounter, and wakes clear-headed and horrified at what he did while dominated. Powerful reunion scene with Mira.
  • Not freed, but Us'gath is destroyed anyway: He collapses with the rest, wakes with the others, same disorientation. Slightly less cinematic but still a full recovery.
  • Killed in the fight: Play this as a genuine tragedy — Mira's arc ends in loss rather than triumph. Valid, darker ending; make sure it's a result of real choices/dice, not railroaded.

Epilogue beats to offer

  • The clan holds a quiet ceremony to properly close the ritual this time — with the party's help, if they want to be involved.
  • Mira formally sponsors the party for advancement past Rank F.
  • A loose thread for a future adventure: was the Hollow Chorus unique, or is it a scout for something larger still rooted deeper in the Understep?

People of Note

Mira Suncoat

Tabaxi Druid, Sunclaw Outrider, age ~17

Earnest, a little too honest for her own good, desperate to prove she belongs. Idolizes her brother Kestrel without reservation — that idolization is the emotional engine of the whole one-shot. She can wild-shape into a modest cat form (flavor only, or a minor mechanical assist at your discretion) and is a capable but unremarkable fighter — she needs the party, and knows it.

Voice: quick, run-on sentences when nervous; goes quiet and clipped when talking about her brother.

Kestrel Suncoat

War Talon of the Sunclaw Clan, Mira's older brother

Before the ritual: warm, protective, a little cocky — the clan's best fighter and Mira's whole model of what a good Tabaxi looks like. After: precise, cold, obedient to the Chorus's will without visible struggle — which is what makes it unsettling rather than obviously villainous.

Redemption Mechanic

Kestrel fights for Us'gath in the final encounter, but he is dominated, not willing. Once Us'gath drops to half HP, or if the party uses the uncorrupted talisman clue from the lair (touching it to Kestrel or invoking Mira's name/a real shared memory as an action), Kestrel gets a DC 15 Wisdom saving throw at the start of his next turn. On a success, he breaks free, immediately gains one free attack against an adjacent Chorusbound creature, and fights for the party for the rest of the encounter. He can attempt this save once per fight without the clue, or with advantage if the clue/roleplay condition is met.

Elder Vashti Emberclaw

Clan Matriarch, led the ritual

The most senior druid of the Sunclaw Clan and the first to be infected — she led the rite and was the first "voice" the Chorus spoke through. If encountered before the final fight (optional, at your table's pace), she's warm but subtly wrong in the same way as the other leaders: finishing sentences oddly, praising the "blessing" a little too much. She is not the BBEG — she is its first and most thoroughly puppeted victim.

Us'gath, the Hollow Chorus

The entity that answered instead of the Great Lion

Not a demon, not a devil — a sprawling, ancient fungal intelligence that has been dormant beneath the Understep for longer than the clan has existed. The ritual's call was the first strong "signal" it had felt in centuries, and it answered honestly believing it was providing what was asked: guidance, unity, growth. Infection spreads through spore contact — scratches, shared grooming, proximity in dens — and each infected host becomes a partial voice/sense-organ of the whole.

Its physical body in the final fight, Us'gath, the False Lion, is the clan's stone Great Lion idol consumed and reshaped by fungal growth into a rough approximation of the real thing. See the stat block for combat mechanics.

Dominating Chorus (Passive). Any creature it has infected (including Kestrel) that starts its turn within 60 ft. must succeed a DC 14 Wis save or act under Us'gath's control that turn (DM's discretion on specifics — usually "attack the nearest non-infected creature").
Resistance. Resistant to poison damage; immune to the frightened condition.
Adds. Runs alongside 1–2 Chorusbound Clanguards and/or a dominated Kestrel (treat as a Clanguard with a better weapon: +6 to hit, 1d8+3 slashing, until freed — see Kestrel's redemption mechanic).

HP and to-hit are tuned for a deadly-but-winnable finale against 4 level 4 characters. Scale HP down to ~70 for a levels-3 party, or if the fight is running long, drop Engulfing Spores' recharge to 6 only.

Clues & Handouts

The Ritual Mural

Faded ochre paint on stone: a ring of Tabaxi figures, arms raised, facing a great maned shape rendered in gold leaf. But beneath the gold lion, painted later, in a darker and cruder hand, something else reaches up from underground root-shapes to meet their hands halfway.

What it tells the party: the clan's rite was answered by something from below, not above — and someone, at some point, noticed and tried to paint a warning without saying so outright.

The Cocooned Chamber

Along the chamber's curved wall, shapes that were once clan members are folded into thick fibrous webbing, fur dulled, chests rising and falling far too slowly. None of them are Kestrel or Vashti. Whatever this is, it hasn't finished with all of them yet.

Use for: the tonal pivot into horror. These clanfolk can be saved afterward if Us'gath is destroyed (see Act 5).

The Uncorrupted Talisman

A carved bone talisman, identical in style to Mira's own, found half-grown-over near the cocoons. Fresh druidic bone/wood seems to resist the fungal growth around it. Mechanically, this is the item that grants advantage on Kestrel's redemption save (see Kestrel's entry) — the party doesn't need to fully understand why it works, just that it visibly does when tested against a patch of fungus.

DM Tips

  • Protect the pivot. Act 1–2 should feel warm and a little funny (giant cat rides!). Don't let horror imagery leak in early — save the cocoon reveal for Act 4 so the tonal shift actually lands.
  • Kestrel is the emotional core. Even players who don't care about Mira will likely care about not having to kill a redeemable NPC. Telegraph the redemption option subtly in Act 3 (a flicker of his old self in his eyes) so it doesn't feel unearned in Act 4.
  • Running long? Cut the Fight 1 (grove floor) encounter in Act 4 down to a single Myconid, or skip straight to Us'gath after the clues.
  • Running short? Add a short roleplay scene with Elder Vashti before the ambush — she's unsettling in a quieter way than the fight, and buys time.
  • Voice consistency. Chorusbound NPCs occasionally sync their speech mid-sentence, then catch themselves. Use this sparingly — twice is eerie, five times is comedy.

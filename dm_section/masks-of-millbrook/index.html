The Masks of Millbrook

A 3.5-hour one-shot for 4–5 characters, level 3–4. Tone: cozy small-town mystery at the start, curdling into paranoia and grief as the party realizes how much of the town they've already trusted isn't real.

Premise

A small cell of doppelgangers has spent the last month quietly replacing residents of the mill town of Millbrook — including the innkeeper everyone loves, Old Maren. The real townsfolk aren't dead; they're held in the Old Millhouse outside town while their doubles study them closely enough to be convincing. The party is hired by Garrick Ostler, whose daughter went "off" a month ago, to find out what's happening — not realizing the warmest, most helpful person guiding their investigation is the thing they're hunting.

⏱ Timeline 🎭 The Hook 🏠 Old Maren 🃏 The Villain 🎲 DM Tips
Core Truth (players should not know this up front) This cell isn't scouting for conquest and it isn't malicious for its own sake. Doppelgangers who are discovered are almost always killed on sight, everywhere they go — so this cell isn't infiltrating Millbrook to exploit it, it's trying to become it, permanently, well enough that they never have to run again. Old Maren genuinely believes that if she's careful and patient enough, she and her people can just... stay. Live real lives. That doesn't make what she's done to the real Maren, Corin, and Elsbet okay — but it's worth playing sincerely in Act 5, not as a mustache-twirl.

Prep Checklist

  • Keep the Doppelganger / Real status column in the People section DM-only — never say it out loud, only show it through the tells.
  • Have 4–5 pregens ready at level 3 if this is a first session for new players.
  • Decide how you'll voice Old Maren consistently warm for ~2 hours before the reveal — the twist only lands if the players genuinely liked her first.
  • Reward: Garrick offers 25 gp/PC up front, another 25 gp/PC on success, plus free lodging at the (real) Sleeping Otter Inn for a tenday.

Session Timeline

Budgeted for a 3.75-hour (~225 minute) session — a little longer than a bare-bones mystery because the Detour earns its keep as a red herring and a non-doppelganger fight. Adjust live — if Act 2 runs long, trim the optional filler fight in Act 4 rather than the investigation or the Detour.

Act 1 · ~10 min

The Hook — Garrick's Plea

The party is passing through, or already resting, at the Sleeping Otter Inn when Garrick Ostler, the town miller, corners them.

Read Aloud "Please — everyone in this town says I'm imagining it, that my Elsbet just needed some time to herself, but I raised that girl. I know her. And the person in her room isn't her. Not really. I don't know how to say that without sounding mad, but I'll pay you to just look. That's all I ask."

Garrick isn't the only one who's noticed something — four townsfolk have quietly changed over the last month — but he's the only one willing to say it out loud, and he's easy to dismiss as an overprotective father. The job is undervalued precisely because it sounds unhinged, which is exactly why it's still sitting open for a new adventuring party to take.

What Garrick Knows (and will share if asked)

  • His daughter Elsbet came back from a errand into the woods about a month ago acting subtly wrong — quieter, oddly agreeable, forgetting small things she'd never forget.
  • Around the same time, the blacksmith Corin stopped taking real commissions, which is deeply unlike him.
  • He doesn't suspect Old Maren at all — she's been nothing but kind, checking in on him constantly since Elsbet changed. (This is the point.)
  • He offers 25 gp/PC now, another 25 gp/PC on success, and free milling services for a year.
Act 2 · ~35 min

Meet the Town — Everything's Fine

Millbrook is warm, small, and ordinary. Let the party shop, chat, and settle in before anything feels wrong — the more genuinely pleasant this act is, the harder Act 4 lands.

Read Aloud Millbrook is the kind of town where nothing happens — smoke curling lazily from the mill's chimney, the Sleeping Otter's sign creaking in the wind, a few townsfolk nodding politely as you pass. It's exactly the kind of place you'd want to retire to. That's what makes Garrick's fear so easy to wave off.

Old Maren's Hospitality

Old Maren greets the party like old friends within minutes of meeting them — free meals, a good room, and unprompted local gossip that's always just true enough to keep their trust. She'll gently point them toward Garrick, toward Tomas, and eventually toward the old millhouse — always steering just slightly away from herself.

Example at the Table

DM (as Maren): "Oh, don't you dare pay for that stew, sit, sit — you've had a long road, I can tell." She's already refilling a mug before anyone asks. "Now — Garrick send you? Poor man's been beside himself. I keep telling him Elsbet just needs some space, girls that age get like that, but between us" — she leans in, voice dropping — "I did think it odd, her missing the Founding Day supper. She never misses that."

Player: "Do you think something happened to her?"

DM (as Maren): "I think — and don't repeat this, I don't want to start a panic — I think you should talk to Tomas before you write him off as just the town drunk. I don't believe everything he says. But I believe some of it."

Notice what she's doing: nothing she says is false, and every line points the party toward real leads. That's how she stays trusted for two more hours.

DM Riffing Notes — Playing Maren She should never lie outright if she can avoid it — she deflects, changes the subject, or answers with something technically true. If a player uses a lie-detection effect on her early, have her dodge smoothly (excuse herself, answer a different question) rather than fail the check. The reveal needs to happen at the millhouse, not get spoiled by a lucky Zone of Truth in this act.

The First Cracks

Give the players small, cumulative details rather than one big reveal — let them piece it together over the session:

  • "Elsbet" doesn't react when Garrick calls her "Bumblebee," her childhood nickname — see Tell 2.
  • "Corin" has let a half-finished commission sit untouched for weeks, deeply unlike the best smith in three villages — see Tell 1.
  • Tomas Wick, the town drunk, keeps insisting he saw "two Elsbets" and gets laughed out of every conversation.
  • Brother Aldwin mentions, almost offhand, that attendance at evening prayers has quietly dropped for exactly the people acting strangest.
Act 3 · ~45 min

Pulling Threads — Investigation & the First Tell

This act is the mystery's engine: three or more clue sources, all roads leading to the Old Millhouse.

The Ostler Mill (Elsbet's home)

Read Aloud The mill smells of flour and damp wood. Elsbet's room is tidy — too tidy. A faint grey residue, almost like ash mixed with jelly, is smeared along the windowsill, as if something climbed in rather than through the door.

A PC proficient in Nature or Arcana (DC 12) recognizes the residue as unnatural, though not necessarily as doppelganger ichor unless they've encountered one before — see Clues & Handouts.

Tomas's Testimony

Read Aloud "I know how it sounds. But I saw her — Elsbet — walking up to her own house, calm as you like, while she was already inside baking bread. Same face. Same walk. Nobody believes me 'cause I drink. Fine. But I know what I saw."

Tell 1 — Catching "Corin"

Tell 1 — Skill fumble: if the party asks "Corin" to do simple smithing (fix a hinge, reshoe a horse), he hesitates, grips the hammer wrong, or claims his hands are "still sore." The real Corin was known as the fastest, best smith in three villages. This is a clean, low-stakes way to confirm a doppelganger exists before the higher-stakes reveal in Act 4.
Example at the Table

Player: "Hey, while we're here — think you could true up this axe head? It's pulling a hair to the left."

DM (as "Corin"): He takes the axe, turns it over once, twice — a beat too long. "...Busy week. Leave it, I'll get to it." He sets it down farther from the forge than a smith normally would, and doesn't meet anyone's eyes.

If a PC has passive Perception 12+, mention privately that his hands don't have a single forge-burn scar — odd for a smith supposedly fifteen years at the anvil. Don't call for a roll; just tell the player who's watching closely.

On confrontation, don't let this one die here. If the party presses "Corin" and he's caught, have him bolt rather than fight to the death — a short chase, not a kill. Seeing a doppelganger revert to true form (Tell 3) is the session's biggest gut-punch, and it should happen exactly once, at the climax, not get spent early on a lesser cell member. If the party corners him anyway, see his entry in People for a mercy option instead of a kill.

Tell 2 — "Bumblebee"

Tell 2 — Missing memory: when Garrick or another local calls "Elsbet" by her childhood nickname, "Bumblebee," she doesn't react, or reacts a beat too late. She also won't recognize an old friend's running joke about the mill's stuck gate.
Example at the Table

Garrick: "Bumblebee! There you are, sweetheart, I was —" He stops. Waits. The nickname just... sits there.

"Elsbet" (half a second too late): "...Sorry, Da. Long day." She doesn't ask what he was about to say. The real Elsbet always did.

Play this quiet — no dramatic sting, no music cue. Let the players decide for themselves whether they noticed, and let it sit unresolved if they don't ask about it directly.

The Detour — Maren's Suggestion

Once the party has a Tell or two and is clearly closing in, Old Maren offers a lead of her own — sincerely enough that it doesn't feel like a deflection: "You know, now that you mention it... I heard some of the new mill hands talking strange, down at the millworks by the river. Might be nothing. Might be worth a look." It isn't a lie exactly — she's simply pointing them at the one place she knows is completely unconnected to her, buying the cell another half-day to prepare.

DM Riffing Notes — Why This Works This is the one earlier place in the session Maren can outright mislead the party without breaking her "never lies" rule too badly — she's not fabricating a threat, just redirecting attention to a real, mundane place. It should feel like a reasonable lead, not an obvious red flag, or the party will get suspicious of her too early.
Read Aloud The road to the new millworks follows the river north, away from town and into thicker woods. It's an easy enough walk — until the birds go quiet all at once, and a low whistle answers from somewhere off the road that definitely wasn't a bird at all.

Ambush: a small band of Millbrook Road bandits — real, mundane, entirely unconnected to the doppelganger plot — has been shaking down travelers on this stretch of road for weeks, a local problem nobody's gotten around to solving. This fight is a deliberate palate cleanser: no shapechanging, no mystery, just a straightforward combat encounter to stretch newer players' legs between roleplay-heavy scenes. See the stat block for scaling.

The New Mill — A Dead End

Read Aloud The new millworks is loud, busy, and utterly ordinary — waterwheel turning, sacks of grain stacked to the rafters, a miller named Hobb wiping flour from his hands and squinting at you like you've wandered in looking for trouble that doesn't exist here. "Strange talk? Lads always talk strange after too much cider. That's the whole mystery, I'm afraid."

There's nothing here. Let the party feel a little foolish, and a little more urgent about not wasting more time — a good gut-check before Act 4 raises the stakes for real.

Finding the Real Lead

Back in town, the actual trail was in front of them the whole time: interrogating a caught doppelganger, re-reading Garrick's ledger (which places every disappearance within days of a "traveling merchant" passing through a month ago), or simply taking Tomas seriously enough to ask where he saw the "two Elsbets" walk — all point to the long-abandoned Old Millhouse a half-mile outside town, not the working mill downstream.

DM Riffing Notes — Handling Wrong Guesses If the party accuses an innocent townsfolk (or worse, Tomas or Aldwin), have the NPC react with hurt or offense, not violence, unless the party escalates first. Keep the social cost low — new players shouldn't feel like they "lost" for guessing wrong.
Act 4 · ~40 min

The Old Millhouse — Where They're Kept

An abandoned mill outside town, disused since a newer one was built closer to the river.

RoomWhat's There
1 — Entrance / Guard RoomFenwick, a real, ordinary human paid to keep watch and whistle a warning — not a doppelganger. Stealth, bribery, or a short non-lethal scuffle; see his entry below.
2 — Holding CellsThe real Maren, Corin, and Elsbet, weak but alive, plus Petyr the fisherman (shaken, alive) and the body of Willa Thorne — see below.
3 — The Ichor Pool ChamberThe leader's lair — see Act 5.
DM Riffing Notes — Why the Guard Isn't a Doppelganger Keep the "reverts to true form on death" reveal (Tell 3) rare and saved for the climax — if the party fights and kills two or three doppelgangers on the way in, the big unmasking of Old Maren loses its punch through repetition. Making the entry guard a real, ordinary person also raises a nice quiet moral question before the fantastical one: will the party hurt an innocent bystander just doing a paid job, who has no idea what he's actually guarding?

Willa Thorne — What Happened to Her

Willa was the cell's first victim, taken almost two months ago — a full month before Elsbet, Corin, or anyone else. Maren chose her deliberately: a solitary seamstress, well-liked but without close family who'd notice small inconsistencies right away. She was meant to be the safest possible test case while the cell was still learning how to hold someone long enough to copy them convincingly.

It went wrong. Held longer than any of the others — nearly six weeks in a cramped side-chamber, with little food and only the doppelganger studying her for company — Willa eventually pieced together what was happening to her: a slip of the tongue from her own "reflection" during a study session, or simply too much time alone to think. She tried to scream and fight her way out. A younger, more panicked member of the cell (see DM Tips for whether you want to name and stat this NPC, or leave it abstract) killed her before Maren, who was away in town maintaining her own cover, could stop it. It is the one death in this whole affair that Maren did not choose and has never forgiven herself, or her own people, for.

Read Aloud Willa is laid in the farthest corner of the holding cell, wrapped in her own unraveled sewing thread as if someone tried, clumsily and too late, to make her comfortable. Beside her, half-finished, is a small christening gown — pale linen, delicate stitching, clearly meant as a gift. It will never be finished now.

Handout — the unfinished gown: if the party asks around town afterward, Widow Hask (see Ordinary Townsfolk) tears up immediately — Willa had been sewing it for the miller Hobb's newborn granddaughter, a surprise nobody in Millbrook knew about yet. This is a good gutpunch to deliver in the epilogue rather than in the lair itself; let the horror of the gown speak for itself in Act 4, and land the emotional weight of who it was for back in town during the resolution.

DM Riffing Notes — Willa and Maren's Monologue Willa's death is the crack in Old Maren's self-justification. When she says she "never wanted anyone hurt" in her monologue, a sharp player may call out Willa by name. Let her flinch — genuinely, not performatively — before she answers. She doesn't have a clean excuse for Willa. That's the point.

Tell 3 — True Form

Tell 3 — Reverts on defeat: any doppelganger reduced to 0 HP or killed reverts instantly to its true grey-skinned form. If this happens to an NPC the party trusted, play the silence afterward — it's the session's biggest "oh no" moment.

The Betrayal

However the party clears Rooms 1–2, Old Maren is waiting in Room 3 — not fleeing, not hiding. She's been expecting them since roughly the moment they started asking questions in town, and she'd rather have this conversation on her own terms.

Read Aloud The old grinding room has been hollowed out. A shallow pool of grey, faintly glowing ichor fills the center of the floor, rippling though nothing has touched it. And standing beside it, smiling the same warm smile she's given you all night, is Old Maren.
Act 5 · ~60 min

The Ichor Pool — Climax & Resolution

Playing Old Maren's Voice

Warm, tired, sincere — not a monster-movie villain. She isn't hiding contempt for the "real" people of Millbrook; she wanted, genuinely, to become one of them. That's what makes this act land as tragedy as much as combat.

Old Maren's Monologue

Read this in pieces as the fight develops — a line when she's first unmasked, more as she takes damage, the rest if she's about to fall.

"You have no idea what it's like, being killed on sight in every town that ever learns what you are. Not judged. Not exiled. Killed, on sight, no matter how long you'd lived there, no matter how many of them you'd fed or bandaged or buried. So yes — I took her face. I took her whole life. But I was good at it. I remembered her recipes. I worried about her father the way she would have. Is that nothing to you?

I never wanted the ones I replaced to suffer. I wanted them kept safe until I didn't need to keep pretending anymore — until this town was mine enough that I could stop looking over my shoulder. That's all any of us ever wanted. Somewhere to finally stop running.

So go on. Take it all apart. Prove, one more time, that there was never going to be a place for us anywhere."

If a player mentions Willa by name, let Maren stop mid-speech.

"...Willa was never supposed to happen. That was never — I wasn't there. I want you to know I wasn't there. That doesn't undo it. I know that doesn't undo it." (A beat. She doesn't finish justifying herself this time.)

The Fight

Old Maren, the Elder Doppelganger plus one standard Doppelganger (the Room 1 guard, if it survived, or a second lesser cell member). See DM Tips for scaling this down for a cautious table.

DM Riffing Notes — Mid-Fight Twist Once per fight, have Maren use Mask Swap (see her stat block) to briefly take the appearance of a PC — purely visual chaos, not a mechanical change — for one gloriously confusing round.

Mercy Option — "Corin" and "Elsbet"

If the lesser doppelgangers are given a real chance to surrender rather than being attacked on sight (see their entries under Corin and Elsbet), they break down and beg not to be killed — they never wanted to hurt Garrick, and in "Elsbet's" case, genuinely grew fond of the role over the month she played it. This is your table's version of a redemption arc: not every doppelganger has to die for the story to resolve.

If Old Maren Is Destroyed

The cell scatters or is defeated with her. The party finds the real Maren, Corin, and Elsbet in Room 2 — alive, disoriented, deeply grateful. Willa cannot be saved; play her loss as a genuine cost of the mystery running as long as it did before the party arrived.

Maren's Real Ledger (Found in Room 3)

Tucked beneath a loose floorboard near the ichor pool is a small, water-stained leather journal — the one document Old Maren kept for herself instead of destroying. Most of it is in plain Common; a few entries slip into a private shorthand of her own invention (DC 15 Investigation or Linguistics to fully decode). Don't gate the emotional weight behind the check, though — the gist reads clearly even untranslated. Read whichever entries fit your table's pace; you don't need all of them.

Handout — Selected Entries

Thornwick Hollow, six years past. Stayed four seasons. A child noticed first, the way children always do — something about how I laughed not matching how I used to. We left before the torches came. I do not let myself think of Thornwick.

Bellcaster, four years past. Longer, that time. Seven of us, woven properly into the town — a magistrate, two guardsmen, a baker, myself. An outside inquisitor found us, not the town. We fought. Four of us didn't leave Bellcaster. I have not tried a town that size again.

The Reedmarsh Camps, two years past. Too small to be worth staying. Wore three faces in a single season and left no one behind me. The easiest year I've had in a decade. Also the loneliest.

Millbrook, these last two months. The longest deliberate stay I've allowed myself in ten years. Small enough to learn completely. Far enough from any city that no one comes looking. I am Maren more hours of the day now than I am myself. I did not expect that to feel like relief.

Undated, the hand shakier than the rest. An outsider asked too many kind questions today. I used to always be able to tell the difference between someone who is merely curious and someone who has already decided what they'll find in me. I could not tell, this time. I don't know if that means I've gotten worse at this. Or if it means some part of me finally wants to be caught.

Use for: a gut-punch coda if the party reads it after the fight, and a built-in hook for a sequel — Thornwick Hollow, Bellcaster, and the Reedmarsh Camps are all real places you can seed elsewhere in your world, each with its own leftover doppelganger presence (or absence) waiting to be explored.

Epilogue Beats to Offer

  • The real Maren reopens the Sleeping Otter within the week — a quietly emotional, low-key scene if the party stops back in before leaving town.
  • Garrick pays out in full and throws in a standing invitation to Millbrook, seeding future adventures.
  • If the party brings Willa's unfinished gown back to town, Widow Hask recognizes it and confirms it was meant for Hobb's newborn granddaughter — a quiet, devastating gut-punch beat to close on before moving to lighter epilogue business.
  • A loose thread for later: was this cell alone, or was it a scout sent ahead of a larger group still looking for somewhere to finally settle?

People of Note

Garrick Ostler

Miller, quest giver — Real

Anxious, grieving, and easy to dismiss as an overprotective father — which is exactly why nobody else in town has taken him seriously. His daughter Elsbet was replaced about a month ago. He doesn't suspect Old Maren at all, which is the whole point of her cover.

Voice: apologizes for how he sounds, then says the true thing anyway.

"Elsbet" Ostler

Miller's daughter — Doppelganger

Quiet, a little too agreeable, a lesser member of the cell. Unlike Old Maren, she isn't senior enough to fully mask the wrongness — see Tell 2. If cornered and given the chance to surrender (rather than being attacked on sight), she breaks — she's grown genuinely fond of Garrick over the month she's spent as his daughter, and never wanted him hurt. See the mercy option.

"Corin" Ashwell

Blacksmith — Doppelganger

Gruff, avoids the forge whenever possible — see Tell 1. A lesser cell member with less emotional attachment to his role than "Elsbet"; more likely to flee or fight than surrender, but the option should still be on the table if players offer it.

Old Maren

Innkeeper, the party's trusted guide — Doppelganger (cell leader)

Warm, endlessly helpful, and the emotional engine of the whole session — she feeds the party real (if selective) clues for two hours before the reveal. She has almost no tell before then: she never eats or drinks in front of guests, always "already ate" or "stepping out back," and deflects rather than fails any lie-detection attempt (see DM Riffing Notes). Full combat stats at Old Maren, the Elder Doppelganger.

Tomas Wick

Town drunk — Real

Rambling and ignored by everyone, but the only person in Millbrook who actually saw a swap happen. Good for an unprompted nudge if the party stalls — he'll seek them out again if they haven't followed up on his story.

Brother Aldwin

Village priest — Real

Calm and genuinely kind — deliberately kept uninfected so the table has at least one NPC they can trust without a second thought, rather than spiraling into suspecting everyone. Can offer healing support if the party recruits him for the final fight.

Fenwick the Lookout

Millhouse entrance guard — Real

A destitute drifter Old Maren pays in coin and hot food to sit near the Old Millhouse and whistle a warning if anyone approaches. He has no idea what's actually inside — he's been told it's a "family dispute" and paid well enough not to ask twice. Cowardly rather than dangerous: he'll flee or surrender the moment it's clear he's outmatched, and knows nothing useful about the doppelgangers beyond "the nice innkeeper pays me to sit here."

Voice: nervous, apologetic, over-explains himself. Wants no part of a real fight.

Willa Thorne

Seamstress, first victim — Real (deceased)

Full details, including how and why she died, are under Act 4, Room 2 and Clues & Handouts. She's the session's grief beat — the proof that this cell's patience has limits, and that Old Maren's claim to have "never wanted anyone hurt" isn't quite true.

The Ordinary Townsfolk

Not everyone in Millbrook is a doppelganger, a victim, or plot-relevant — most of the town is exactly what it looks like. Use these for texture, comic relief, and to keep the party from spiraling into suspecting literally everyone.

  • Widow Hask — runs the market stall, gossips constantly, entirely real, entirely harmless. Will absolutely try to sell the party "lucky" charms at triple price.
  • Pip — a miller's boy, maybe nine, obsessed with the idea that the party are "real adventurers." Offhandedly mentions his friend's mum "doesn't do the voices for bedtime stories anymore," which is either nothing or a clue, depending on whether the DM wants to plant a fourth household of interest.
  • Old Fennick — half-deaf retired miller, remembers everyone's business from thirty years ago and none of what happened yesterday. Good comic-relief red herring who confidently accuses the wrong person of "acting strange" (they're just having a bad week).

Clues & Handouts

The Grey Residue

Smeared along Elsbet's windowsill: cool, faintly damp, somewhere between ash and jelly. A PC who later sees a doppelganger revert to true form (Tell 3) will recognize this as the same substance — a satisfying "click" if you let them make the connection themselves rather than stating it.

Tomas's Testimony (handout)

"I saw her walk into her own house while she was already inside it. Same face. Same walk. I know how it sounds."

The Disappearance Ledger

Garrick's own careful notes (he's the type to keep records) show all four disappearances cluster within a few days of a "traveling merchant" passing through Millbrook a month ago — the doppelganger cell's arrival.

Willa's Unfinished Gown

A small christening gown, half-stitched, found beside Willa's body in Room 2 — see her full story. Nobody in town knows yet who it was for; that reveal lands best back in Millbrook during the resolution, not in the lair itself.

DM Tips

  • Protect the pivot. Acts 1–2 should feel genuinely cozy. Don't let paranoia leak in too early — save the real dread for Act 4 so the reveal actually lands.
  • Old Maren is the emotional core. Even players who don't care about the mystery mechanics will care about having trusted her. Keep her warm and consistent for as long as possible before the unmasking.
  • Deliver tells through roleplay, not checks. Say what the NPC does rather than gating it behind a die roll — reward attentive players without punishing distracted ones.
  • Running long? Cut the optional filler fight in Act 4 entirely.
  • Running short? Add a short scene with Brother Aldwin in Act 2 — quiet, unsettling in a different register than the fights, and buys time.
  • Mercy matters. If players offer "Corin" or "Elsbet" the chance to surrender, let it land as a real moral beat, not a wasted turn — see the mercy option.
